Modified VGG-19 Architecture

Python | TensorFlow | Keras | Scikit-Learn

The
Problem

  • Potatoes are a staple food crop in the Philippines and play a crucial role in addressing food security challenges and serves as a significant source of income for many Filipino farmers. However, the industry grapples with challenges like potato diseases, which can hinder production and profitability.

  • Precision agriculture offers a modern solution for achieving efficient, productive, and sustainable farming, but general-purpose models like VGG-19, while effective for broad image recognition tasks, often fall short when applied to specialized agricultural datasets.

  • Potato leaf diseases exhibit subtle, localized features such as discoloration, spots, and texture changes, which generic architectures may not capture effectively.

The Potato Leaf

Sample of images from the potato leaf dataset. From left to right: Healthy Leaf, Early Blight, Late Blight

The potato leaf images used for the training and testing of the models was sourced from 2 sources. One was from PLD Dataset on Kaggle, and the remainder was collected by the researchers from Atok, Benguet, Philippines. The dataset consists of 4,082 images, which have undergone image preprocessing to enhance the input quality and improve training efficiency. Of these, 3,251 images were used for training data, 416 for validation data, and 415 for testing data (80/10/10 data split). The dataset was divided into three classes: healthy, early blight, and late blight, containing 1,020, 1,638, and 1,424 images, respectively.

The dataset was validated and certified by Agriculture Experts from Provincial Agriculture Office of Zambales, Philippines. (Certificate of Validation)

Sample of augmented images from the potato leaf dataset.

The images from the potato leaf dataset were preprocessed by resizing them to a uniform input shape of 224 x 224 pixels in RGB format and normalizing the pixel values to a range of 0 to 1. Following this, data augmentation techniques were applied to enhance the dataset's diversity. These augmentations included random rotations, flipping (horizontal and vertical), zooming, repositioning (shifting), adjusting brightness, and introducing noise, all aimed at improving the model’s ability to generalize and perform effectively on unseen data.

The total size of the augmented dataset, generated through data augmentation techniques and used for training and testing the models, is approximately 97,500 images.

Baseline vs Modified


To assess the effectiveness of the modifications made to the VGG-19 architecture, a comparative analysis is conducted between the modified VGG-19 model and the baseline VGG-19 model. This comparison evaluates whether the proposed changes result in significant improvements in the model's performance for potato leaf disease classification. Both models were trained and tested on the same dataset under identical conditions.

The Rationale


Why the VGG-19 Architecture?

VGG-19 was selected based on the conclusion of extensive experiments and research studies, which consistently identify it as one of the most superior deep convolutional neural network (DCNN) architectures. Notably, a study by Ghosh et al. provided a comprehensive analysis of three advanced DCNN models—VGG-19, DenseNet121, and ResNet50—for recognizing and classifying potato leaf disease images. Their findings revealed that DenseNet121 achieved the highest accuracy of 97.92%, followed by VGG-19 at 92.71%, and ResNet50 at 92.67%.

However, upon conducting a similar experiment, I discovered that VGG-19 was the best-performing architecture among the three. The VGG-19 model achieved a test accuracy of 87.15%, outperforming DenseNet121, which scored 81.04%, and ResNet50, which achieved 77.74%.


The Modifications

The modifications made to the baseline VGG-19 architecture includes the introduction of a Global Average Pooling (GAP) layer, reducing the dimensions of the dense layers, adding dropout layers, incorporating both L1 and L2 regularizations, monitoring the validation loss, and checks for the learning rate. It also has included image preprocessing, and image augmentation to achieve better generalization.

The GAP was introduced to reduce spatial dimensions which forces the model to focus on the most significant features instead of details that might not be important. It is beneficial for smaller fully connected (FC) layers because it simplifies the transition between the convolutional layers and the classification layers, reducing the overall number of parameters in the fully connected layers.

The dense fully connected layers were reduced in dimension because through extensive experimentation and trial and error, we discovered that the large number of parameters in the baseline VGG-19 architecture contributed to the overfitting observed by the study of Ghosh et al. This is due to the fact that the problem at hand was not overly complex — the model was tasked with classifying only three classes with each having relatively simple features, which made it so that less parameters is better for better generalization.

Furthermore, the reduction in the size of dense layers helps mitigate overparameterization, which in turn reduces the risk of overfitting. Fewer neurons in the dense layers lead to faster training and inference times, which is especially crucial when deploying models on devices with limited resources. This model was developed with a clear goal in mind: to help precision agriculture and improve the food security in the country. The model was ultimately used in a mobile application prototype which hopefully can be a stepping stone in modernizing agriculture in the Philippines.
